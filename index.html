Justice Amy Coney Barrett

Personal Life


Amy Coney Barret was born in New Orleans, Louisiana with 5 sisters and a brother. She went to an all girls high school and then went to Rhodes College majoring in English Literature. She graduated named most outstanding English student. Then she went to Notre Dame Law School on a full-ride scholarship. She graduated Law school with a summa cum laude. In 1999 she married Jesse M. Barret and they had 5 children and adopted 2. She is also a Catholic.

Career


Barrett spent two years as a judicial law clerk after law school, first for Judge Laurence Silberman of the U.S. Court of Appeals from 1997 to 1998, and then for Justice Antonin Scalia of the U.S. Supreme Court from 1998 to 1999. From 1999 to 2002, Barrett practiced law at Miller Cassidy Larroca & Lewin, a boutique law firm for litigation in Washington, D.C. In 2001, Barrett was a visiting associate professor and John M. Olin Fellow in Law at George Washington University Law School. Her academic work has been published in the Columbia, Cornell, Virginia, Notre Dame Law Review, and Texas law reviews.

Being Appointed

On May 8, 2017, President Donald Trump nominated Barrett to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit — the federal appellate court covering Illinois, Indiana, and Wisconsin — after Judge John Daniel Tinder took senior status. Barrett was on Trump's list of potential Supreme Court nominees since 2017, almost immediately after her court of appeals confirmation. Her appointment was surrounded by controversy as she was elected near the end of Donald Trump's term

Landmark Cases

Department of State v. Munoz (2024)
Topic: Immigration & National Security
A citizen does not have a fundamental liberty interest in their noncitizen spouse being admitted to the country.

Lindke v. Freed (2024)
Topic: Free Speech
When a government official posts about job-related topics on social media, this speech is attributable to the government only if the official possessed actual authority to speak on the government’s behalf and purported to exercise that authority when they spoke on social media.
